The Poverty Rate in Kebumen Regency is the Highest in Central Java.


Kebumen, Bhumi Tirta Praja Mukti (Land and Water that can generate prosperity), is still far from expectations. The poverty rate in Kebumen in 2022 remains the highest in Central Java. Poverty alleviation programs continue to pose a challenge to the region.

Various policy programs implemented have not yet significantly reduced the poverty rate. It is essential to adopt approaches that align with the characteristics of poverty to optimize poverty alleviation efforts.

In-depth studies on the poverty profile based on regional clusters and an evaluation of the effectiveness of social assistance are needed to formulate poverty alleviation strategies in Kebumen Regency.











The Dynamics of Poverty in Kebumen Regency

The poverty alleviation program in Kebumen Regency has achieved relative success in recent years and deserves appreciation. This is evident from the reduction in the poverty rate from 2012 to 2019, although there was an increase in 2020 and 2021, both in terms of numbers and percentages.
The increase in the poverty rate is a consequence of the COVID-19 pandemic. The poor population, vulnerable individuals, and those working in the informal sector were the most affected by the pandemic's spread. Various mobility restrictions and economic activities have led to a decrease in income, causing an increase in poverty as more people have fallen below the poverty line in terms of average per capita monthly expenditure.
In 2022, there was some encouraging news as the number and percentage of the poor population decreased, even lower than in 2019 (before the COVID-19 outbreak). The number of poor people in 2022 in Kebumen Regency decreased to 196.19 thousand individuals (16.41 percent).



The distribution of the welfare levels among the population in Kebumen reveals an imperfect pattern. In the year 2022, the percentage of poor and vulnerable-to-poverty populations in Kebumen Regency stood at 13.26 percent and 10.98 percent, respectively.

Furthermore, more than half of the population is already on the path to the middle class. This condition can be leveraged by the Kebumen Regency Government in formulating poverty alleviation program policies. The population on the brink of the middle class can serve as a stepping stone for those in poverty to move towards the vulnerable category by creating employment opportunities for them.

On the other hand, the Golden Indonesia Vision for 2045, which targets a population of the upper-middle class and above to be 70 percent, faces relatively significant challenges. This can be observed from the economic growth effects in Kebumen, which have been less focused on the middle-class population, as depicted in the image above.
The above graph illustrates changes in household consumption expenditure levels from 2015 to 2022, categorized into 10 levels of prosperity. Code 1 represents the poorest group, while code 10 represents the richest group. Household consumption expenditure, both on food and non-food items, for the group moving towards the middle class during the period 2015-2022, experienced a decrease of 1.29 percent.
Food consumption expenditure reflects income, as a consequence of the economic growth in Kebumen. This also implies that the development occurring in Kebumen Regency is not accompanied by an increase in income for the group moving towards the middle class.
Indeed, the population transitioning toward the middle class is expected to eventually become part of the middle-class society and contribute to investments supporting SMEs in Kebumen Regency. Therefore, the local government needs to pay attention not only to the impoverished population but also to encourage those moving toward the middle class to ascend. The local government should revamp its poverty alleviation strategies in Kebumen to make the programs more effective.

Profile of Poverty in Kebumen by Regional Clusters


The efforts to alleviate poverty in Kebumen Regency are regulated by Regent's Regulation (PERBUP) of Kebumen Regency Number 31 of 2014 regarding the Criteria and Procedures for the Poverty Population Census. It divides Kebumen Regency into five regional clusters based on local conditions and regional wisdom. These clusters include urban, coastal, coastal mountain, mountain, and bonorawan clusters.

The profile of the impoverished population in the urban cluster generally includes a heterogeneous range of occupations, with a higher proportion in the service sector. Their education levels are relatively low, resulting in limited skills and generally low wages. Social assistance serves as a lifeline and hope for escaping poverty, with some residents even using it as a form of debt collateral. The impoverished population relies more on educational financial aid for their children as a means to improve their economic prospects in the future.

The provision of social assistance has a negative impact, as it tends to reduce work ethics due to excessive reliance on the provided social assistance.

The profile of the impoverished population in the coastal cluster primarily consists of farmers. The turning point in the coastal region's economy occurs when transitioning from staple crops to horticulture. The social capital of the coastal cluster's residents is robust, with a high spirit of communal cooperation. However, the cultural tradition of contributing during social gatherings becomes a significant expense, even for impoverished individuals. They are willing to incur debt to contribute to residents hosting such events.

Coastal Mountain

Education Low
Occupation Homogeneous
Impact of Social Assistance Effective Only for the Elderly
Poverty Alleviation Efforts Business Innovations
Regional Potential Tourism and Fisheries

The majority of the population in the coastal-mountain cluster work as fishermen, with their capital coming from external wholesalers outside Kebumen. Nevertheless, this occupation is considered high-risk with uncertain outcomes. The residents in this cluster possess strong determination, a high work ethic, and great creativity, which enables them to produce local products such as palm sugar and smoked tuna, as well as engage in livestock farming. Transportation and information access in the community are relatively open, and the residents make use of the existing tourism potential, such as Watu Bale Beach, Lampon Beach, and Patemon Beach, as business opportunities. The groups formed in this cluster represent a social force that can promote a sense of community and form business groups. Direct assistance provided to the community does not alter their behavior; it is only effective for the elderly.

The majority of the population in the mountain cluster primarily work as farmers or independent laborers. The poverty mentality remains a significant issue that needs improvement. Assistance in home renovations (RTLH) has greatly contributed to improving health standards, while sanitation assistance still requires further enhancement. The family's need for clean water and agriculture can be met through the availability of clean water supply (Pamsimas). Most impoverished families in this cluster have inherited their poverty status, leading them to depend on social assistance. The impoverished community acknowledges the importance of education, but costs and access remain obstacles. The majority of the population chooses to migrate to improve their standard of living, even though there is still unused land in the village. This is because there are no successful agricultural examples in the vicinity. On the other hand, the community is developing local potential businesses, such as export-quality palm sugar, traditional cake and empon-empon industries.

Strategies for Poverty Alleviation

In broad terms, there are five recommended strategies for poverty alleviation in Kebumen Regency. These five strategies are as follows.

01
Market Linkage

The market linkage approach is a collaborative effort involving various stakeholders to create and develop businesses in a more equitable and sustainable manner. In general, there are seven principles of market linkage, which include impartiality, capacity building, equity, gender equality, local wisdom, strengthening networks and partnerships, and market orientation.

02
Maintaining Price Stability and Purchasing Power of the Community

The second strategy is implemented through the formulation of targeted assistance policies for entrepreneurs, the establishment of business organizations/institutions to handle the distribution of agricultural products, and the creation of markets/auction institutions to prevent the decline of agricultural commodity prices.

03
Collaborative Multisectoral Data-Based Intervention

Collaborative multisectoral intervention fundamentally emphasizes the pattern of interaction and connectivity between stakeholders and policy-makers. This governance requires an integrative poverty alleviation system and basic services that can link institutions responsible for poverty alleviation programs and beneficiaries.

04
Baseline Assessment by Optimizing REGSOSEK Data

REGSOSEK or the Socio-economic Registration (Registrasi Sosial Ekonomi) data encompasses the overall socio-economic conditions of the entire population in Indonesia, including the level of well-being. This Regsosek data can be optimized as the basis for formulating policies and strategies for social protection and community empowerment to be more targeted. The formulation of these policies and strategies must, of course, prioritize the principles of integration, inclusivity, and equity.

05
Provision of Change Agents through Higher Education Scholarships

This strategy is implemented through the One Village One Product (OVOP) program, utilizing capable and highly educated human resources to harness the region's potential for development.
